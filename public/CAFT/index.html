Aligning Forest and Trees in Images and Long Captions for Visually Grounded Understanding

Byeongju Woo1, Zilin Wang2, Byeonghyun Pak1, Sangwoo Mo3, Stella X. Yu2
1Agency for Defense Development   2University of Michigan   3POSTECH
Paper arXiv Code

TL;DR: We propose CAFT (Cross-domain Alignment of Forests and Trees), a hierarchical image–text representation learning framework that jointly aligns global scenes (forests) with local image regions (trees), enabling fine-grained visually grounded understanding from long captions without any pixel-level supervision.

Abstract

Large vision-language models such as CLIP struggle with long captions because they align images and texts as undifferentiated wholes. Fine-grained vision-language understanding requires hierarchical semantics capturing both global context and localized details across visual and textual domains. Yet linguistic hierarchies from syntax or semantics rarely match visual organization, and purely visual hierarchies tend to fragment scenes into appearance-driven parts without semantic focus.

We propose CAFT (Cross-domain Alignment of Forests and Trees), a hierarchical image-text representation learning framework that aligns global and local semantics across images and long captions without pixel-level supervision. Coupling a fine-to-coarse visual encoder with a hierarchical text transformer, it uses a hierarchical alignment loss that matches whole images with whole captions while biasing region-sentence correspondences, so that coarse semantics are built from fine-grained evidence rather than from aggregation untethered to part-level grounding.

Trained on 30M image-text pairs, CAFT achieves state-of-the-art performance on six long-text retrieval benchmarks and exhibits strong scaling behavior. Experiments show that hierarchical cross-domain alignment enables fine-grained, visually grounded image-text representations to emerge without explicit region-level supervision.

Method

CAFT is built on the observation that long captions have a natural two-level hierarchy: individual sentences describe local image regions (trees), while the full caption summarizes the global scene (forest). Standard contrastive models like CLIP collapse this hierarchy, producing a single image embedding and a single text embedding aligned at only one granularity. CAFT preserves and exploits it via two architectural components and a hierarchical alignment objective.

Fine-to-Coarse Visual Encoder

The visual backbone processes an image from local patch tokens up to regional and scene-level representations. Intermediate feature maps capture fine-grained spatial semantics, while the final [CLS] token aggregates global scene context. This gives the model access to representations at multiple visual granularities simultaneously.

Hierarchical Text Transformer

Long captions are encoded at two levels: each sentence is embedded independently (capturing local, region-level semantics), and the full caption is encoded as a whole (capturing global, scene-level context). The part-whole structure of the text encoder mirrors the fine-to-coarse structure of the visual encoder.

Hierarchical Alignment Loss

CAFT is trained with a two-level contrastive objective. At the global level, the full image embedding is aligned with the full caption embedding — matching forest to forest. At the local level, regional visual features are aligned with individual sentence embeddings — matching tree to tree. The joint loss ensures that global representations emerge from, and remain consistent with, fine-grained regional evidence. Neither level can be learned independently of the other, preventing the model from ignoring local detail or global coherence.

Results

Long-Text Image–Text Retrieval

CAFT is evaluated on six long-text retrieval benchmarks, where models must match images to detailed, multi-sentence captions (and vice versa). These benchmarks specifically stress-test fine-grained visual grounding — the ability to locate specific image regions described in the text. CAFT achieves state-of-the-art performance across all six benchmarks, outperforming prior vision-language models trained on comparable data.

Scaling Behavior

Training on 30M image-text pairs, CAFT demonstrates robust scaling behavior: performance consistently improves as model size and data scale increase. This scalability is a direct consequence of the hierarchical alignment objective — more data provides more diverse region-sentence pairings, enabling the model to learn richer correspondences at both granularities.

Emergent Grounding Without Supervision

Despite never seeing explicit region annotations (bounding boxes or segmentation masks), CAFT learns to spatially ground textual phrases in images. Visual attention maps show that the model spontaneously associates sentences describing specific objects or regions with the corresponding image locations — an emergent property of the hierarchical alignment objective applied at scale.
